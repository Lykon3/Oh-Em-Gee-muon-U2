\begin{abstract} \noindent The persistent discrepancy between experimental measurements and Standard Model (SM) predictions of the muon anomalous magnetic moment (a_\mu)---the Muon g-2 anomaly---has long been viewed as a potential window into physics beyond the Standard Model. In this proposal, we present a refined computational framework in which the anomaly emerges as a residual geometric effect arising from the partial overlap between an inherent, coherent vacuum geometry and discretized lattice QCD computations. We enhance the formalism for quantifying this overlap by incorporating insights from topology (persistent homology, Morse theory) and differential geometry (Ricci flow), and explore connections to adjacent research in AdS/CFT, quantum Hall systems, and recursive collapse manifold architectures. Our framework aims to bridge the gap between discrete numerical methods and the continuous nature of quantum fields, potentially resolving the long-standing anomaly without invoking new fundamental particles or forces.
\vspace{0.5em} \noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Muon g-2 anomaly, Lattice QCD, Vacuum geometry, Hadronic vacuum polarization, Quantum field theory, Persistent homology, Ricci flow, Topological methods \end{abstract}
\section{Executive Summary}
The Muon g-2 anomaly, characterized by a persistent \sim 4.2\sigma discrepancy, may signal not only new physics but also inherent limitations in current computational models. This proposal outlines a comprehensive framework in which the residual discrepancy is viewed as a precise computational artifact: a partial resolution of a coherent vacuum geometry endemic to the quantum vacuum.
We detail a rigorously derived and now enhanced overlap factor between the full geometric contribution and that captured by lattice QCD. This revised formalism considers more general topological structures and their evolution across discretized spaces. Our strategy incorporates: \begin{itemize} \item Empirical lattice results from multiple collaborations \item Advanced numerical geometry techniques (Regge calculus, AdS/QCD) now enriched with topological perspectives (persistent homology, Ricci flow) \item Machine learning approaches for pattern recognition \item Cross-lepton validation studies \item Comparative analysis with insights from AdS/CFT correspondence, quantum Hall systems, and recursive collapse manifold theories. \end{itemize}
Ultimately, this work seeks to redefine our understanding of quantum corrections by reconciling discrete methods with continuous physical phenomena through a topologically and geometrically richer framework.
\section{Theoretical Motivation & Hypothesis}
\subsection{Background: The Muon g-2 Anomaly}
The anomalous magnetic moment of the muon, defined as: \begin{equation} a_\mu = \frac{g_\mu - 2}{2} \end{equation} where g_\mu is the muon's gyromagnetic ratio, has been measured with extraordinary precision. The current experimental world average stands at: \begin{equation} a_\mu^{\text{exp}} = 116,592,061(41) \times 10^{-11} \end{equation}
The Standard Model prediction, however, yields: \begin{equation} a_\mu^{\text{SM}} = 116,591,810(43) \times 10^{-11} \end{equation}
This results in a discrepancy of: \begin{equation} \Delta a_\mu = a_\mu^{\text{exp}} - a_\mu^{\text{SM}} = 251(59) \times 10^{-11} \end{equation}
\subsection{Central Hypothesis}
\begin{hypothesis}[Vacuum Coherence Geometry] The observed discrepancy in the muon anomalous magnetic moment arises from a partial overlap between an intrinsic, coherent geometry of the quantum vacuum and the discretized resolution provided by lattice QCD calculations. This residual geometric contribution---if properly quantified using an advanced formalism incorporating topological and differential geometric insights---could explain the anomaly without invoking additional fundamental particles or forces. \end{hypothesis}
The recent discrepancies in lattice QCD calculations of hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP) effects have prompted critical examination of computational methods. Our enhanced hypothesis posits that the observed discrepancy emerges as a computational residual, governed by the topological and geometric characteristics of how discrete computational grids resolve continuous vacuum structures.
\section{Residual Overlap Factor Derivation: Enhanced Formalism}
\subsection{Conceptual Mathematical Framework}
We model the coherent vacuum geometry as contributing a full geometric term a_\mu^{\text{full geometry}}. Lattice simulations, due to their finite spacing a, capture only a fraction of this. This fraction is quantified by an overlap factor f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a, r), where r is a characteristic length scale (e.g., the muon's Compton wavelength). The anomaly is the residual: \begin{equation} a_\mu^{\text{residual}} = a_\mu^{\text{full geometry}} \cdot \left[1 - f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a, r)\right] \label{eq:residual_orig} \end{equation}
\subsection{Refining the Overlap Factor f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a, r)}
The initial model for the overlap factor, \begin{equation} f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a, r) = 1 - \exp\left(-\frac{r^2}{a^2}\right) \label{eq:overlap_orig} \end{equation} suggests a Gaussian-type suppression. While providing useful boundary conditions (f_{\mathrm{overlap}} \to 1 as a \to 0, and f_{\mathrm{overlap}} \to 0 as a \to \infty), a more sophisticated understanding can be achieved by considering the underlying topological and geometric structures.
\subsubsection{Topological Perspective: Persistent Homology} Instead of a fixed exponential form, we can model the evolution of the overlap factor with lattice spacing a using concepts from persistent homology. This approach quantifies how topological features (connectivity, holes, voids) of the vacuum coherence structure persist or disappear as the scale of observation (related to a) changes.
We can define the rate of change of the overlap factor with respect to the lattice spacing as a function of the homological persistence: \begin{equation} \frac{d}{da} f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a, r) = -\Phi(\mathcal{H}(a,r), r) \label{eq:persistent_homology_f} \end{equation} where \mathcal{H}(a,r) is the homological persistence function. This function encodes how essential geometric coherence structures, characterized by their homology groups H_k(\mathcal{M}_{\text{vac}}), are resolved or lost on a discretized manifold \mathcal{M}_{\text{lattice}}(a). The function \Phi captures the rate at which unresolved features contribute to the residual.
Further refinement can be achieved using Morse theory, where critical points in the discretization process (e.g., where lattice cells merge or split in a way that alters resolved topology) dictate transitions in geometric coherence. The persistence weights in \Phi could be derived from the density and nature of these Morse critical points.
\subsubsection{Discrete Curvature Constraints and Ricci Flow} The overlap factor can also be understood through the lens of discrete differential geometry. If the residual anomaly arises from a local geometric deficit not captured by the lattice, the evolution of this deficit can be related to how curvature is represented and evolves on the discrete lattice.
The Ricci flow equation, \begin{equation} \frac{d}{dt} g_{ij} = -2R_{ij} \end{equation} (where t can be conceptualized as a flow parameter related to refinement or scale), naturally governs deformations of geometric structures. A discrete analogue of Ricci flow (e.g., Forman's Ricci curvature or Ollivier's Ricci curvature on graphs) could model how f_{\mathrm{overlap}} changes as the lattice adapts or is refined. The "time" evolution parameter t could be linked to 1/a^2 or a similar measure of resolution. The overlap factor would then be an integral of this flow, reflecting how much of the "ideal" continuous curvature is matched by the discrete representation. \begin{equation} a_\mu^{\text{residual}}(a) = a_\mu^{\text{full geometry}} \cdot \mathcal{G}\left(\int_0^{1/a^2} \mathcal{R}{\text{discrete}}(t') dt'\right) \label{eq:ricci_residual} \end{equation} where \mathcal{G} is a function that maps the integrated effect of discrete curvature evolution (represented by $\mathcal{R}{\text{discrete}}$) to the residual.
\subsection{Implications for Parameter Determination} The original derivation (Eq.~\eqref{eq:overlap_orig}) led to an effective lattice spacing $a \approx 1.36~\text{fm} $ (Eq. \ref{eq:lattice_spacing_orig} below) by matching the experimental residual. \begin{equation} \exp\left(-\frac{r_\mu^2}{a^2}\right) \approx 0.1512 \quad \Rightarrow \quad a \approx \frac{1.87~\text{fm}}{\sqrt{1.889}} \approx 1.36~\text{fm} \label{eq:lattice_spacing_orig} \end{equation} The refined formalisms (Eqs. \ref{eq:persistent_homology_f}, \ref{eq:ricci_residual}) imply that this single "effective lattice spacing" a is a simplified parameter. Instead, the residual would depend on a more complex interplay of topological feature persistence and curvature evolution across scales. The goal would be to derive the functional form of \Phi or \mathcal{G} from first principles or fit them using data from various lattice simulations with differing a and discretisation schemes.

\subsubsection{Wick Rotation Analysis} Investigation of analytic continuation effects on coherent geometric resolution remains crucial.
\subsubsection{AdS/QCD Approaches and Holographic Tensor Networks} Employing holographic duality, as in: \begin{equation} f_{\mathrm{overlap}}^{\text{AdS}} = \int_0^\infty dz , \Psi(z/a) \Phi_{\text{bulk-boundary}}(z, r_\mu) \end{equation} where \Psi(z/a) is a cut-off function related to lattice spacing a, and \Phi_{\text{bulk-boundary}} is the bulk-to-boundary propagator. \subsubsection{Persistent Homology and Morse Theory in Lattice Simulations} We will explicitly compute Betti numbers and persistence diagrams for lattice configurations at different spacings a to empirically determine \mathcal{H}(a,r) and identify critical points corresponding to changes in resolved topology relevant to vacuum coherence.

\subsection{AdS/CFT Correspondence in Discrete Quantum Gravity} As noted in Sec 4.2.3, holographic tensor networks in AdS/CFT offer a compelling parallel. The entanglement structure of the vacuum, represented by the tensor network, is truncated or imperfectly represented when projected onto a discrete boundary theory. The "missing information" or residual deviation in such models could be formally analogous to our a_\mu^{\text{residual}}. The geometry of the tensor network itself, particularly how its connectivity reflects bulk geometry, can inform models of \mathcal{H}(a,r).
\subsection{Geometric Phase in Quantum Hall Systems} In fractional quantum Hall (FQH) physics, topological properties are paramount. Discrepancies or corrections in discretized models of FQH systems sometimes involve Berry curvature corrections. There might be an analogy where the vacuum coherence geometry possesses a non-trivial "Berry phase" analogue, and its incomplete representation on the lattice leads to the residual. Exploring if an effective Chern-Simons-like correction term could parameterize the vacuum coherence decay (or 1 - f_{\mathrm{overlap}}) is a promising avenue. This would involve investigating whether embedding the lattice QCD framework within a background gauge field (representing the unresolved geometric structure) impacts anomaly persistence. Such a term could naturally arise if specific homology cycles (e.g., 3-cycles if d=4) in the vacuum manifold are not properly captured by the lattice.
\subsection{Recursive Collapse Manifold Architectures and Entropy-Weighted Reweighting} Drawing from prior work on recursive geometry and vacuum topology, which has shown connections between vacuum fluctuation stabilization and persistent homology structures, we can significantly enhance the model. The entropy-weighted tensor reweighting framework offers a powerful tool. We propose to extend the overlap suppression model into a multi-layer collapse bifurcation architecture. \begin{itemize} \item The vacuum coherence can be conceptualized as a hierarchy of geometric structures at different scales. \item Lattice discretization acts as a filter, causing "collapse bifurcations" where finer geometric details are lost or mapped to coarser representations. \item The f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a,r) could be a product or sum over contributions from different layers of this hierarchy, weighted by their information-theoretic importance (e.g., an entropy measure related to their persistence or complexity). \begin{equation} 1 - f_{\mathrm{overlap}}(a,r) = \sum_k w_k(r) \cdot \delta_k(a,r) \end{equation} where w_k(r) is an entropy-derived weight for the k-th geometric layer relevant at scale r, and \delta_k(a,r) is the unresolved fraction of that layer by a lattice of spacing a. \item This architecture could naturally identify "phase transition" points (in terms of a or r) where the nature of coherence distortion changes, potentially explaining why certain lattice spacings or methodologies yield different results for HVP. \end{itemize} This connects directly to the persistent homology view, as bifurcations can be tied to the birth/death of homological features.
